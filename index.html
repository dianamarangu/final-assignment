TUBERCULOSIS: Challenging the status quo

By Dr. Nkatha Mburugu, Pulmonologist and Professor, University of Cape Town, South Africa

What do we know?

Tuberculosis (TB) is ancient and deadly

Consumption and the White Plague were words used in the past to refer to tuberculosis (TB), all connoting the deadly nature of this disease. Indeed an ancient disease, the oldest evidence of TB in human beings dates back to over 9000 years ago from a Neolithic settlement in the Eastern Mediterranean.

Advances in bacteriological discovery, cure and prevention have been made

On March 24th 1884, the German scientist, Robert Koch, announced his discovery of the cause of this deadly disease, Mycobacterium tuberculosis. In this knowledge lay the hope for an end to the scourge that had long afflicted the human race. Subsequent advances in TB were made including the discovery of potent antibiotic regimens and laboratory diagnostic techniques. Indeed the world was making progress.

Moving beyond just tackling the bacteria...The WHO STOP TB Strategy was formulated

Despite these steps forward, TB was declared a global emergency in 1990. Strategies recommended by the World Health Organization (WHO) were put in place, including Directly Observed Treatment (DOTS), DOTS Plus and the current STOP TB strategy. Between 1990 and 2012, TB death rates dropped by 45% and WHO estimated that 22 million lives were saved as a result of these strategies.

The STOP TB Strategy has 6 principle components which include:

  • Pursuing high quality DOT expansion and enhancement
  • Addressing TB/HIV, MDR and other challenges
  • Contributing to Health System Strengthening
  • Engaging all care providers
  • Empowering people with TB and communities
  • Enabling and promoting research.

BUT currently 80% of the global burden lies in 22 countries

The number of people falling ill with TB annually is declining. The 2012 WHO global estimates of 8.6 million people sick with TB compared to people in 2011 indicate that the world is on track to achieving the millennium development goal of reversing the spread of TB by 2015. The harsh reality however is that great regional disparities exist. Sixty percent of new TB infections occur in Asia whereas sub Saharan Africa bears the largest proportion of new TB cases per population with over 255 cases per 100,000 population. It is not surprising that this geographical pattern follows that of HIV since TB-HIV co-infected individuals have about a 30-fold increased risk of falling ill with TB. South Africa has not been spared and currently ranks 7th out of the 22 countries with the highest burden of disease - 80% of the global burden.
